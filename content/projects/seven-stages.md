Seven Stages of Enlightenment is a ceramic series composed of seven porcelain spheres, each finished with a distinct glaze formula developed through sustained material research. The glazes originated from an ongoing project focused on glaze chemistry, chromatic development, and the interaction between composition and firing atmosphere. Each sphere functions as both sculptural object and test site: a controlled yet responsive field in which variation in silica, fluxes, alumina content, and kiln conditions generate finishes ranging from vitreous gloss to matte, from fluid movement to crystalline structuring.

The choice of high-fired porcelain is deliberate. It's density, traslucency, and fine particle structure amplify the sensitivity of glaze response, allowing subtle compositional shifts to register visibly and tactically. The uniform geometry isolates the variable of glaze, making difference perceptible through reflection, texture, and depth rather than through changes in form.

Conceptually the series draws from the seven classical stages of the alchemical Magnum Opus: Calcination, Dissolution, Separation, Conjunction, Fermentation, Distillation, and Coagulation. Each sphere corresponds to one phase, translating symbolic processes of breackdown and reintegration into material phenomena. Darkness, opacity, iridescence, crystallization, and luminosity become analogues for psychic and spiritual states. The work proposes transformation not as a linear improvement but as cyclic reconfiguration, matter repeatedly subjected to heat, fracture, recombination, and stabilization.


Technically the series is directly linked to Magnum Opus, where the same glaze formulations operate within a different sculptural framework. Here, however, the spherical format functions as a more distilled meditation on repetition and variation. The sphere, continuous, without hierarchy or orientation, suggests recurrence and return. Each surface records a moment of trasmutation, holding within it the evidence if chemical reaction and controlled risk.


Through the disciplined constraints of form and the volatility of glaze chemistry, Seven Stages of Enlightenment positions ceramic practice as both laboratory and allegory: a site where material transformation becomes a precise, embodied metaphor for processes of internal change.
